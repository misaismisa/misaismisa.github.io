What is Lure?

Lure is the solution to a cluttered all-category search as a more refined search application that focuses purely on beauty related services.


The Problem

While ‘Yelp’ is highly popularized as the number one service search application, its often focused on restaurants and its layout can be confusing for users. After speaking with a number of women using Yelp in regards to searching for beauty-related services, having a more streamlined beauty-focused application, would alleviate the headache of searching for a hair salon.

The Solution

Lure is a crowd-sourced review platform and booking service optimized for mobile. Focusing only on 8 major beauty categories: hair, nail, spa, hair removal, eyebrow, makeup, tanning and barber, it’s a elegant solution to a more chaotic mess.

User Research

A survey was created to research the habits and current applications used by a female-targeted audience.
The following questions were used to focus on specific areas of interest:

  • Which applications/websites are currently being used
  • The top 5 key features users look for when using the application
  • The top 3 frustrations users are currently facing
  • The interest level of a beauty-focused application
  • The services users would like to see presented in a possible application
  • The importance of booking through the application

02

Competitive Analysis

Choosing 3 major mobile applications using a SWOT analysis I was able to compare the features of Yelp, StyleSeat, and Vagaro.

Yelp was the obvious choice as the top competitor. It’s branding and presence among users is widely known and people use the service to search for everything. It’s positioned itself at the top and is backed by its number of reviews and the number of businesses on its application. It does face issues of review manipulation and poses a threat to smaller businesses. StyleSeat and Vagaro were chosen for their focus on beauty-related services. These two services are direct threats to a potential application but the lack of market presence still leaves room for opportunity. They both have a unique design and booking features.

04

User Stories

Compiling and dividing a number of tasks between the new user, returning user, and all users helped me to prioritize and narrow the most important actions users would take.
Some key takeaways included:

  • performing a search
  • booking an appointment
  • leaving a review
  • viewing critical information on a business (e.g. map, photos, reviews)
  • basic functions of the application (e.g. viewing appointments, notifications, bookmarks)

05

User Flows & Sitemap

Gathering my user research and focusing on the most important tasks of my user stories, I came up with 3 major instances in which users would interact with the application. These three include: 1) searching for a business and booking an appointment, 2) signing-in as a returning user/signing-up as a new user, and 3) browsing through the navigational components of the application (search, home, profile, bookmarks, and notifications). I later included a fourth task, leaving a review, after conducting my first round of testing. My sitemap then included a streamlined version of all my user flows and had a simple navigational structure.

Branding Work



RESEARCH AND SKETCHING:

Using brainstorming techniques of mind-mapping and word lists, I tried various combinations of words related to beauty that could be used as a brand name. After much deliberation, the word ‘Lure’ as a spinoff of ‘Allure’ was the most attractive as a name that was unique and trendy.



REFINEMENT:

Defining and designing a logo to accompany “Lure” proved to be quite a challenge. I originally wanted a logo that wasn’t painfully obvious in regards to beauty related services, such as a “pair of scissors” or a “lip”. I chose an eye originally thinking that it could be somewhat gender neutral but received mixed feedback on the iterations I had. After consideration, I decided to scrap the existing logos and chose to refine the word itself and came up with a colorful “Lure” logo design.


COLOR PALETTES:

With my mood-board as my inspiration, I drew my organic pastel mints, peach and grey tones to create a luxurious look. 07

Wireframes

With sketching, I was able to flesh out the basic framework of how I wanted my screens to look. I then used Figma, to build out my digital iterations, I knew that compared to my first project, taking the time to structurally compose each part would save me a lot of time when it came to my high-fidelity mockups. With 43 screens, I was able to build out the main screens for each user flow action. I realized that for certain components, I needed to make sure I understood Apple’s HIG and often toggled back and forth between it’s UI library to copy some of it’s features.

With testing I took a fun approach and made a nifty, cardboard cutout of a “phone” and printed out my digital wireframes. I then tested the wireframes by having the users slide the screens behind the cutout or tap with their finger and I would switch out screens. This really put into perspective the simplicity in which the user needed to interact with the layout of the application, especially when I had my 8-year old niece test.

08

Mockups

Using my digital wireframes, I then constructed my high fidelity mockups in Figma, which was useful in helping me quickly build out design components and focus on color and imaging rather than layout. The more challenging parts of this task were making sure that I was displaying information in a concise way and also fleshing out the search function of my application. Making sure the flow was direct, easy to use, and aesthetically pleasing. I spent a lot of time on my hi-fis constantly learning as I went (with a lot of referencing to Yelp and Pattrns.com) on how to elevate the appearance of my designs and the hierarchy of information.



09

Prototypes and User Testing Results


PREFERENCE TESTING:

With my first preference test, I wanted to test to see which homepage wireframe layout would be the most intuitive to users. I had about 77% select the first option of choice. With regards to the second preference test, I had users test for hierarchy of information on my search feature. I realized through several iterations that I needed to keep it simple and lose the map behind it, as it was getting very little real estate. My third preference test included the login page which tested to see how users wanted to toggle between login and signup screens.




CLICKABLE PROTOTYPE TESTING:

Putting together a tappable prototype via Marvel, I connected it with Maze to have remote users test for the 4 tasks I had developed earlier. 1) As a new user, search for a hair salon and review its information 2) Sign-up for a new account and book the first appointment 3) Leave a review on a past appointment as a returning user 4) Look at bookmarks and notifications. I also tested on 2 live users in-person to see the actual flow and structural integrity of the application.

I received significant feedback which included:

  • Making the user to have to tap on category filters before selecting the first choice salon, made it confusing when I was giving brief instructions
  • Some users felt it made more sense to tap on the stylist to review their information before selecting a time
  • Leaving a review was confusing for users because they had multiple routes they could have gone down when I had one option for them to select
  • Making “Reviews” a major component of the navigation and merging “Home” and “Search” as one
  • Having the user with the option to “Logout” from the new user stage, would have made for an easier transition into logging in as a ‘returning user’ to complete certain tasks
  • Greying out certain elements on the page would have it made more clear to the user on how to approach certain tasks

Taking this feedback in mind, I went back to my mockups and edited the booking section to have a separate stylist page which would show the options for time under each individual. I did a overhaul on my navigation and assessed which tasks were most important, eliminating “home” so that “search” could be the main home nav component and adding in “reviews” as a navigation option. I also assessed how complicated my prototype was and made sure to simplify the flow for the user.

// LESSONS LEARNED


With this project being my second run, it taught me some valuable lessons as I progressed in my abilities to conduct research, analyze, test, and design.

+ Stick to your target.

Nothing proves more true when you’re working through research or design and a wrench is thrown in to leave you confused and back at square one. This was the case when I was coming up with a logo design and received varying feedback on my designs. I changed my audience to be more inclusive towards the opposite sex and felt that I had to create a logo that was “neutral” in a sense, but felt lost when I was drafting up versions with barber-related symbols. I needed to remind myself that my original intention was to have women as my target audience and create the best MVP possible. I could then later adjust what is now my “Lure” logo to have colors that were less “feminine” and more vibrant to match a more gender-neutral audience.

- Don’t spend time on screens you won’t be using

As I design my high-fidelity mockups, I often realize, “Oh wait! I also need to design these screens, otherwise the users will feel like they have suddenly jumped from one to another.” However, this can also lead me down a rabbit hole, chasing after other screens on my application that aren’t necessary to the user tasks that I want my users to test. Knowing that in the future, I won’t have the luxury of time, I have to learn to scale back the amount of time spent on designing my mockups, putting together the necessary screens for my prototype, and learning my tools thoroughly to save me from pixel pushing every single pixel, every screen at a time.

With more time...

I would like to build out a basic information screen that the first-time user would have to fill out before booking an appointment. I realized that the application wouldn’t have any information on the user unless it grabbed data from another application. Conducting more research on “map” versus “list” presentation would also be useful in figuring out what was important to the user, as well as whether having one icon, a “heart”, to represent bookmarks and starred would be more effective than separating the two into different categories.
