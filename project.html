What is Cloudcore?

As part researcher and designer, it was important to understand the client’s objectives while assessing the audience’s needs. Information was provided to start with a cloud storage service that would have specific features: saving, organizing and creating content, uploading files, and sharing. Using a number of different tools, among them Sketch, Invision, Illustrator, and prototyping paper, I was able to construct the basic framework and final designs for Cloudcore.


The Problem

To build a cloud storage service that serves to address the needs of users who are finding frustrations with applications in the current market. These frustrations include confusion with locating an item or action on the website, the organization of the site, and the capacity of storage. The site focuses on uploading and sharing content, with some creation and organization elements combined.

The Solution

Cloudcore is a SaaS (Software as a Service) web and mobile application, for both professional users and the everyday user. It’s fun and colorful design makes it appealing to users who are looking to store items for personal use. Yet, its simplistic and minimal layout is attractive for professionals who need to collaborate and share work with others.

User Research

A survey was used to conduct research on the uses for cloud storage services and the current habits and perspectives of current users.

The questions used in the survey focused on the following areas:

  • Which current services users are using
  • The 5 most appealing features of the site
  • The 5 least appealing features of the site
  • The primary use for these applications
  • The importance of collaboration and sharing content

Competitve Analysis

For the competitive analysis, I decided to compare 3 major sites using a SWOT analysis.

Dropbox has been a longtime contender and has been a standard service used by many businesses. Google Drive has been gaining traction with its productivity suite with its many individual and business users. Both present large hurdles to overcome to be at the top of the cloud storage game. I chose Pinterest, in that, the client wanted to integrate a more organization market feature. Pinterest is strong in its visual representation of items and organization. It's simple and easy to use. An integration of the features of all 3 of these applications makes for an interesting service.

User Stories

After compiling a large list of tasks to accompany the new user, returning user, and all users, I prioritized these tasks in order of importance from high to low. High priority tasks were limited to the essentials, which included signing in, upgrading the account, and uploading a photo to more menial tasks limited to items such as logging out, exploring payment plans and collaboration.


User Flows & Sitemap

After looking back through the survey results, user personas, and user stories, I came up with 4 situations/instances where a user would spend majority of their time using the application. The four included: signing-up/logging-in, uploading content, organizing content, and sharing content. I created user flows for each of these stories which are combination of high and mid to low-level tier. Comparing the original sketches with that of the digital versions, there was a lot of reiteration and simplifying the flows, so that processes were more straightforward. From there, I created a streamlined sitemap that would encompass these flows.

Branding


Branding Work


Research and Sketching

For my cloud storage project, I wanted to think of a brand name that would be easy to remember and straightforward. Looking at competitors, I also noticed that “Google Drive”, “Dropbox”, “Box”, “Cloudup”, and “OneDrive” are all examples of current services on the market with easy to decipher names and branding.

Ultimately, I came up with “CloudCore”. The name is straightforward and encompasses the meaning of being the foundation of storage or at the center of all cloud storage services.


Refinement

Looking over my sketches, one image and name stuck out to me in particular. An image of a cloud encased in a circular wave to accompany the name “CloudCore”. The name itself felt it needed a rounded feeling and I played around with several different logo concepts, with the cloud placement in various positions. I ultimately came up with the final design below. For the typography, I knew I wanted something clean, simple and slightly rounded to match the logo design. My final decision ended with Quicksand and Open Sans as both my primary and secondary font styles.


Color Palettes and Styleguide

For my color palette and style guide, I took a look at the colors and styles being used on my mood board and came with a variant of teal as my primary color to accent the monotone white, grey, and black with a contrasting berry color as the primary action button. Blue represents strength and reliability and darker greens representative of stability and affluence. Both of these colors combined bring a rich hue of teal which I think is a good balance between the standard blues that are commonly associated with cloud storage services and a new color that differentiates it from the rest while not deviating too far into a different color realm.


Wireframes

I originally started out sketching 4 pages based on my user flows and sitemap. As I was building out the digital wireframes, I came to realize that the flow of user actions needed for more pages as the user delved deeper into the site. Signing-in as an action, simply did not stop at one page but rather 7-8 pages worth of wireframes. In total, I came to a total of 27 pages for my desktop version and 17 for my mobile application.



Execution


Mockups

The mockups were an extensive revision and design of my wireframes. I spent a lot of time and constant reiteration on the design, color, typography and alignment of the high-fidelity mockups. It became a learning process of finding the right balance between different elements of the page, how the natural and intuitive layout of a website worked and how the user would either come to face frustration or ease of use with the site. Building out the mobile application after the website, made me realize how much more direct and simple designing the website thereafter would have been.



Prototypes and User Testing Results


Preference Testing

For my preference tests, I chose 3 different elements between my desktop and mobile versions. The tests consisted of first, a comparison between a rectangular and rounded button style, the second between two toolbar styles for the mobile application, and inputted color text. Results ultimately revealed that users preferred the rounded button style, a more emphasized toolbar style, and a dark grey font as inputted text.




Clickable Prototype Testing

I decided to test 3 people (2 in-person and 1 remote) for my wireframes and high fidelity mockups. For the first round of testing, I focused on the desktop wireframes. The second round included both my mobile and desktop high-fi mockups. I came to several conclusions after finishing the tests and reviewing the videos. First, that the ease and use of both applications were straightforward and simple. Most encountered minor issues. Second, that language is important. The need for concise and clear wording helps the user to understand what the next action should be. I had the users test 3 tasks: signing up for an account, upgrading the account to the next tier, and uploading a link or image to the dashboard.

I received great feedback on both versions, which led to multiple changes and adjustments to achieve the final product:

  • the use of language (e.g. “sign up” over “get started” is more clear and concise)
  • the ability to click in user forms so that the flow of actions is easier
  • emphasis on color, sizing, and wording so that the action stands out to the user
  • a more simple and direct navigation
  • variations on styling of buttons, modal windows, and boxing to create hierarchy
  • obvious actions were missing such as “sign out” or being able to exit a window
  • mobile is clearer and sets the foundation for a simplistic structure





Lessons Learned


Looking back on all the steps of the UX Design Process for this cloud storage service, I realized that as my first major project, constant reiteration and testing was important to understanding and finding the issues and needs of users. To develop another cloud storage web service in an already competitive market, I had to pinpoint the strengths of existing ones and attempt to add and organize details where they were lacking. In-person testing was key to understanding how users viewed and approached the site, as well as communication through the use of language and style. Color also played an important factor in limiting which were really essential as “call-to-action” items versus accent colors to avoid confusion. I realized that developing the mobile application first would have provided a better structure for me in building out the web application and less frustration and time spent on fixing portions of the site.


If I had more time, I would go back and revise my web application to match the features and styles of my mobile application and conduct more research on how users would prefer to have information presented to them, both in style and language. The following steps for revision would include: assessing the secondary colors and the primary actions colors and refine them to be more concise throughout the site, make the elements on mobile and web consistent (e.g. dates to appear), and creating a grid view for different viewing.
